Module 1
Money — Basics, Functions & Types
What money is, why we hold it, and how to classify it.
🪙 What is Money?
Definition: Money is anything widely accepted for buying/selling goods or paying back loans. It eliminates the need for barter (e.g., exchanging rice for shoes). A 50-taka note buys bread — no one needs to trade rice. That's money as a medium of exchange.
💡 Four Functions of Money
🔄
Medium of Exchange
Used to buy/sell goods & services. You pay 100 BDT for a T-shirt instead of giving rice.
📏
Unit of Account
Measures & compares value. Book A = 500 BDT, Book B = 700 BDT → B is costlier.
⏳
Standard of Deferred Payment
Pay later. Take an education loan now and repay in the future.
🏦
Store of Value
Keeps value for future use. Save 10,000 BDT today and spend it next month.

🏦 Demand for Money — 3 Motives
💳
Transaction Motive
For daily expenses. Rafique keeps 20,000 BDT for food, travel, bills.

Income ↑ → Transaction demand ↑
🩹
Precautionary Motive
For emergencies. Saves 10,000 BDT in case of a sudden doctor visit.

Higher risk perception → more held
💹
Speculative Motive
For investment. Keeps 20,000 BDT waiting for share prices to fall.

Interest rate ↑ → Speculative demand ↓
💴 Legal Tender vs Non-Legal Tender
✅
Legal Tender
Must be accepted by law for all payments. Example: Bangladeshi Taka. You cannot refuse BDT as payment within Bangladesh.
❌
Non-Legal Tender
Optional — not required by law. Example: Foreign currency, promissory note. A shopkeeper can legally refuse USD payment.
🔁 Velocity of Money
How fast money circulates in the economy.
Example: A 1,000 taka note changes hands 5 times in a month (restaurant → supplier → worker → shop → transport) → Velocity = 5.

High velocity = active economy | Low velocity = people saving or not spending.
📊 Nominal vs Real Interest Rate
🏷️
Nominal Rate
The printed interest rate from banks. Ignores inflation.
Example: Bank offers 5% interest.
📉
Real Rate
Actual gain after adjusting for inflation.
Formula: Real = Nominal − Inflation
If inflation = 2% → Real = 5 − 2 = 3%
Negative Real Rate (Stagflation): If Nominal = 4%, Inflation = 6% → Real = −2%. Your money's purchasing power is shrinking even with "interest."
💸 Money vs Credit
ConceptMoneyCredit
MeaningMedium of exchange (cash, deposits)Borrowed funds repayable with interest
SourceEarned or savedBorrowed
ExampleCash, bKash balanceLoan, credit card dues
Price of money = Interest rate — decided by demand & supply of money in the market.
⚙️ Crawling Peg Exchange Rate
Crawling Peg: Exchange rate changes gradually based on market conditions or inflation, not suddenly.
Example: Bangladesh slowly devalues Taka: 117 → 118 → 119 BDT/USD to stay competitive in exports. No sudden shock — exporters can plan ahead.

🧮 Worked Example — M1 & M2 Calculation
Given data:
Currency in circulation: 2,923.60 | Currency in tills of DMBs: 241.80 | Demand deposits: 1,837.40 | Other checkable deposits: 6.20 | Time deposits: 13,054.30 | Money market mutual fund: 10.50 (all billion taka)
M1 = (Currency in circulation − Currency in tills) + Demand deposits + Checkable deposits
= (2923.60 − 241.80) + 1837.40 + 6.20 = ৳4,525.40 billion

M2 = M1 + Time Deposits + Money Market Fund
= 4525.40 + 13054.30 + 10.50 = ৳17,590.20 billion
📉 Worked Example — Broad Money & Time Deposits
Given: M1 = ৳450 crore; M1 = 30% of M2
→ M2 = (450 ÷ 30) × 100 = ৳1,500 crore

Time Deposits = M2 − M1 = 1500 − 450 = ৳1,050 crore

Demand deposits = 60% of M1 = 0.60 × 450 = ৳270 crore
COB (Currency outside banks) = M1 − Demand Deposits = 450 − 270 = ৳180 crore
🧮 How Commercial Banks Create Money (Multiple Deposit Creation)
Banks don't just hold deposits — they multiply them by lending!
  • You deposit 1,000 BDT → Bank keeps 100 BDT (10% reserve) and lends 900 BDT.
  • Borrower spends 900 → Seller deposits 900 → New bank keeps 90, lends 810 BDT.
  • Process repeats… → Total money created ≈ 4,645 BDT from 1,000 BDT originally!
Money Multiplier (MM) = 1 ÷ Reserve Ratio (RR)
If RR = 10% → MM = 1 ÷ 0.10 = 10
Every 1 taka of reserve creates 10 taka in total deposits.

Single bank creates: Deposit × (1 − RR) = 1,000 × 0.80 = ৳800 (if RR=20%)
Entire system creates: (1 / RR) × Deposit = 10 × 1,000 = ৳10,000
🧮 Monetary Aggregates After Transactions (99th BPE — Page 27)
Initial (31 Dec 2023): COB = ৳85,000 | M₁ = ৳2,35,000 | M₂ = ৳7,45,000
TransactionCOBM₁M₂Logic
(i) Encash FDR ৳1,00,000 → buy Sanchaypatra ৳50,000 + deposit ৳50,000 to current a/c85,0002,85,000 ↑6,45,000 ↓FDR (M₂) → current a/c (M₁). Sanchaypatra also M₂ so M₂ drops by full FDR.
(ii) Withdraw ৳15,000 from savings → keep as cash1,00,000 ↑3,00,000 ↑6,45,000Shift within M₂ (savings→cash). M₂ unchanged; M₁ ↑ as cash is more liquid.
(iii) Cash-out ৳20,000 from MFS agent (bKash)1,20,000 ↑3,00,0006,45,000Currency ↑ but demand deposit ↓ by same amount. M₁ net = no change.
Final (1 Jan 2024): COB = ৳1,20,000 | M₁ = ৳3,00,000 | M₂ = ৳6,45,000
Rule: If money becomes MORE LIQUID → M₁ ↑. If it stays in long-term deposits → M₂ only changes.
🔧 Bangladesh Bank Tools to Control Inflation
📈
Increase Repo Rate
Loans become expensive → less borrowing → less money in market. If inflation = 9%, BB raises repo from 6% → 7%.
🏛️
Sell Govt Bonds (OMO)
Banks buy bonds using cash → money is taken out of circulation → money supply falls.
🔒
Increase CRR
Banks must hold more cash → lend less → reduces money supply and credit creation.
🪙 Determinants of Money Supply
⚡
Monetary Base (High Powered Money)
Cash in circulation + bank reserves at Bangladesh Bank. This is the foundation of money supply.
✖️
Money Multiplier
How many times money expands due to lending. MM = 1 ÷ RR. Higher RR → smaller multiplier → less money created.

Payment Systems
Evolution, types, advantages, Bangladesh-specific systems, and BPE topics.
📜 Evolution of Payment Systems
Stage 1
Commodity Money — Gold & silver coins. Mughal era kings used gold. Valuable but heavy & hard to carry.
Stage 2
Paper Currency (Fiat Money) — Govt-issued notes. BDT, USD, INR. Backed by trust & law, not gold. Light & easy.
Stage 3
Cheques — Written bank order. Good for large transactions (house rent ৳25,000) without carrying cash.
Stage 4
Electronic Payments — Cards, BEFTN, bKash, QR codes. Bangladesh: EFT introduced 2010, BACPS + BEFTN modernized transactions, MFS = financial inclusion.
💳 Types of Payment Methods — Pros & Cons
💵 Cash Payments
Advantages

  • Instant, no processing
  • Universally accepted
  • Helps budget control
Disadvantages
  • Can be lost or stolen
  • Hard for large payments
  • No digital record
  • Risk of fake notes

  • Safer than large cash
  • Good for B2B payments
  • Works without internet

  • Instant transactions
  • Contactless & hygienic

Disadvantages

  • Needs smartphone & internet

  • Battery drain issues
🔲 QR Code Payments
Advantages

Disadvantages

⚙️ Key Bangladesh Payment Infrastructure
⚡
RTGS
Real-Time Gross Settlement. Instant, individual settlement for high-value transfers (corporate, govt). Run by Bangladesh Bank. No batch delay.
🔄
BEFTN / ACH
Bangladesh Electronic Funds Transfer Network. Batch processing of salary payments, vendor transfers. Introduced 2010. Not real-time like RTGS.
🏧
BACPS
Bangladesh Automated Cheque Processing System. Modernized cheque clearing. Combined with BEFTN to transform Bangladesh's payment infrastructure.
💳 Taka Pay Card (98th & 99th BPE)
Domestic digital card introduced by Bangladesh Bank.
Works for QR, debit/credit, mobile wallet payments. Goal: promote cashless Bangladesh & reduce foreign payment dependency (Visa/MasterCard fees). Not a rival to international cards — a localised alternative.
💬 SMS Banking — Push vs Pull
📤
Push Messages (Bank → Customer)
Bank initiates the message.
Examples: Salary alert, ATM withdrawal notice, loan reminder, cheque dishonour notice.
📥
Pull Messages (Customer → Bank)
Customer initiates the request.
Examples: Type "BAL" to check balance, mini statement, fund transfer, bill payment query.
🚧 Challenges of E-Payment in Bangladesh (97th BPE)
  • Weak infrastructure — poor internet and power supply in rural areas.
  • Low awareness and digital illiteracy among rural population.
  • Cyber security threats — hacking, phishing, SIM fraud.
  • Inter-bank transfer delays and settlement risks.
  • Weak regulatory framework for digital finance.
  • Limited merchant acceptance outside cities.
Solution: Upgrade network infrastructure, digital literacy campaigns, stronger cyber laws, better inter-bank protocols & regulatory updates.

Financial System

📘 What is a Financial System?
Definition: A complex network of institutions, markets, instruments, and intermediaries that channel funds between savers and borrowers, enabling investment, capital formation, and economic growth.
🔹 Three Types of Finance
🏘️
Rudimentary Finance
Traditional economy with only money, no financial assets. No banks, no bonds — just barter or cash. Example: village cash-only economy.
➡️
Direct Finance
Savers lend directly to borrowers by buying securities. No intermediary needed. Example: buying govt bonds, company IPO shares.
🏦
Indirect Finance
Transfer through intermediaries (banks, insurers). You deposit → bank lends to borrower. Example: BRAC Bank giving SME loan from your savings.
🔹 Constituents of a Financial System
📄
Financial Instruments
Claims of one party over another. Examples: shares, bonds, CDs, mutual funds, T-Bills, LC.

All money is a financial instrument, but NOT vice versa.
🏛️
Financial Institutions
Provide financial services. Banks, insurance, MFIs, pension funds. Bridge savers & borrowers.
📊
Financial Markets
Venues where instruments are traded. DSE, money market, capital market, FX market.
🔹 Types of Financial Markets

🔹 Perfect Market vs Efficient Market
Perfect Market
  • Many buyers/sellers, full information
  • No monopoly, no cost barriers
  • All participants have equal access
VS
Efficient Market
  • Prices reflect ALL available info
  • Mispricing short-lived
  • Asset price = fair value based on info

🔹 Are Banks Dying? No — They Are Transforming.
Market share is shrinking due to fintechs & NBFIs — but banks are not disappearing. They adapt by: offering fee-based services (mutual funds, insurance), charging for previously free services, partnering with fintechs, entering joint ventures. Banks remain special because: they are highly regulated, they create money, and bank failure can destabilise the entire economy.
🔹 Fund Management — Two Approaches (ALM)
Pool of Funds Approach
All sources (deposits, borrowings, capital) → combined pool → distributed to reserves, loans, investments. Simpler but may not optimise risk/return.
VS
Conversion of Funds Approach
Each source matched to a specific use to minimise risk & maximise return. Short-term deposits → short loans; long-term deposits → long loans. More sophisticated.

🪙 Repo vs Reverse Repo (97th BPE)
Repo (Repurchase Agreement)
Bank SELLS securities (e.g. T-bonds) to BB and agrees to buy them back later at a slightly higher price. Price difference = interest. Used to get liquidity (short-term borrowing tool).
VS
Reverse Repo
Central bank BUYS securities from commercial banks to absorb excess liquidity and control inflation. Banks deposit money with BB effectively.
📈 Capital Market — Long-Term (> 1 Year)
Trades long-term securities > 1 year. Purpose: channel savings into productive investments, facilitate long-term fund mobilization. Examples: DSE, CSE (Bangladesh), NYSE (USA).

🔄

Module 7
Islamic Financial System
Shariah principles, banking instruments, Bangladesh context, and Sukuk vs bonds.
🌙 What is Islamic Finance?
A system based on Shariah principles — forbids interest (Riba), uncertainty (Gharar), and unethical activity. Promotes risk-sharing & social justice. Goal: Fair, ethical finance benefiting both parties and society.
📜 Core Principles
🚫
No Riba (Interest)
Charging/paying any interest is haram. Profit comes only from risk-sharing, not guaranteed returns.
⚠️
No Gharar (Uncertainty)
Excess uncertainty or speculation prohibited. Contracts must be clear and transparent.
🤲
Zakat
Obligatory 2.5% charity to redistribute wealth to the poor. Reduces wealth inequality.
🕌
Vice-Regency
All wealth ultimately belongs to Allah; humans are trustees responsible for ethical use.
📚 Sources of Shariah Law
📖
1. Qur'an
Primary & eternal source of guidance. Highest authority in all matters.
🕌
2. Hadith
Sayings and actions of Prophet Muhammad (pbuh). Second source after Qur'an.
🤝
3. Ijma
Consensus of Islamic scholars on issues not explicit in Qur'an or Hadith.
⚖️
4. Qiyas
Analogical reasoning applied to new situations similar to established rulings.
💫 Relationship Between Finance & Religion (Ismail 1992)
❤️
Aqidah
Faith and belief — the foundation of all Islamic conduct including finance.
📋
Shariah
Practical actions: Ibadat (worship duties) + Muamalat (social/economic transactions — the finance part).
🌟
Akhlaq
Morality and ethics in conduct — governs HOW financial transactions are done.

🇧🇩 Islamic Banking in Bangladesh
Islami Bank Bangladesh Ltd (IBBL) — first interest-free bank in Southeast Asia, established 1983.

🏦 Sources & Uses of Funds (Islamic Banks)
Sources of Funds

Al-Wadiah deposits — Bank as trustee; refundable on demand; no return promised.

Mudaraba deposits — Depositors = investors; bank = manager; profits shared; loss by depositor unless negligence.
Uses of Funds
Murabaha — Bank buys asset → sells with markup (car, equipment).

Bai Mu'ajjal — Deferred payment credit sale to client.

Ijara-bil-Bai — Lease then sell on deferred basis (lease-to-own arrangement).
💰 Money as "Potential Capital" — Why No Riba? (98th BPE)
In Islam, money is a medium of exchange — not a commodity. It produces a return only when combined with productive economic activity.
Trading money for money (charging interest) = Riba → prohibited.

Why the prohibition? Riba is unearned, exploitative profit. It encourages financial speculation over real economic activity, and concentrates wealth unfairly.

🏦 Central Bank — Definition & Objectives
A central bank is a public institution that controls a country's money supply and credit. In Bangladesh → Bangladesh Bank, established under Bangladesh Bank Order 1972.
#ObjectivePurpose
1Price StabilityControl inflation via interest rate, OMOs, reserve ratios
2Financial StabilitySupervise banks, prevent bank runs, act as lender of last resort
3Economic GrowthStable environment for investment and job creation
4Full EmploymentLower rates to encourage business borrowing and hiring
5Exchange Rate StabilityMaintain stable currency to facilitate trade & investment
⚙️ Functions of Central Bank
📜
a) Note Issue
Sole right to issue & manage currency. Maintains price stability and controls money supply in circulation.
🏛️
b) Banker to Government
Custodian of govt funds. Processes payments. Short-term loans against T-Bills. Advises on financial matters. Manages public debt.
🏦
c) Banker's Bank
Commercial banks keep mandatory reserves (CRR) with BB, enabling monetary control.
⚖️
d) Clearing House
Settles inter-bank claims via cheque and account entries maintained at the central bank.
🆘
e) Lender of Last Resort
Provides emergency funds to banks facing liquidity shortages by rediscounting approved securities.
💱
f) Forex Operations
Manages reserves of foreign currency, gold, and assets to stabilize the local currency (BDT).
🧮 Credit Control Methods
A. Quantitative Methods — affect overall credit supply
MethodExplanation
Bank Rate PolicyRaising rate → restrict credit (anti-inflationary). Lowering → expand credit (pro-growth).
Open Market Operations (OMO)Buying govt securities → ↑ money supply. Selling → ↓ money supply.
Variable Reserve Ratio (CRR/SLR)Higher ratio → banks lend less. Lower ratio → banks lend more.
B. Qualitative / Selective Methods — target specific sectors or behaviours
MethodExplanation
Rationing of CreditRestricts total amount available per borrower or sector
Direct ActionPenalises banks that violate credit policy
Moral SuasionInformally persuades banks to follow policy (guidance without rules)

💰 CRR vs SLR (97th BPE)
TermFull FormDefinitionExample
CRRCash Reserve Ratio% of total deposits banks MUST keep as reserve with Bangladesh BankCRR=5% → bank keeps ৳50cr for ৳1,000cr deposits

Both ensure liquidity and prevent over-lending. BB CRR minimum = 5.5% | SLR minimum = 13% of demand and time liabilities.

💱 Free Float vs Managed Float (97th & 98th BPE)
Free Float
Currency value determined entirely by market forces (supply & demand). No govt interference. Example: USD, Euro.
VS
Managed Float
Mostly market-based, but central bank occasionally intervenes to stabilize. Example: India, Bangladesh. BB buys/sells USD to control BDT volatility.
📊 Financial Interrelation Ratio (FIR) & FIMR (97th BPE)
RatioMeasuresExample
FIR (Financial Interrelation Ratio)% of financial assets (loans, securities, cash) to total assetsFinancial assets = 800 of 1,000 → FIR = 80%
FIMR (Financial Intermediation Ratio)% of total assets used for lending/intermediationLoans = 700 of 1,000 → FIMR = 70%
Higher FIR/FIMR = stronger banking activity and credit flow in the economy. Good sign of financial development.
🏛️ Central Bank Independence (97th BPE)
⚙️
Operational Independence
Freedom to set interest rates and implement monetary policy without political pressure. Most crucial for effective policy.
🎯
Goal Independence
Freedom to set objectives like inflation targets. Needed but less common in practice — govts often set the mandate.
Why it matters: Independent central banks maintain lower inflation, greater stability, and long-term credibility. Political interference leads to money printing and inflation spikes.

📉 HTM vs HFT Securities
⏳
HTM — Held to Maturity
Long-term investments (e.g., 10-year govt bond) held until maturity. Unaffected by daily market price changes. Stable income, no trading gains/losses.
⚡
HFT — Held for Trading
Short-term investments (company shares) meant to sell quickly for profit. Market value fluctuates daily. Higher risk, potential quick gains.
👥 Principal-Agent Problem
Occurs when the agent (manager) acts for personal gain instead of the principal's (owner's) interest.
Example: A branch manager approves risky loans to meet targets and earn a bonus — short-term gain for him, long-term risk for the bank and depositors.

Solution: Internal controls, independent audits, and performance evaluation aligned with long-term organisational goals.

💼 Trust-cum Settlement Account
Used by stockbrokers or agents to hold client money temporarily for financial transactions. Keeps clients' funds separate from the broker's own funds. Used only to complete deals (buy/sell of shares, bonds). Ensures transparency & investor protection.
